# The Paper Chase — Skeleton & Encounter/Benefit Ledger

Analysis of [`../source/paper-chase-refactor.md`](../source/paper-chase-refactor.md).
This is a working analysis to drive a revision — **not** a rewrite. It pulls the
whole story down to its spine, lists every adventure moment, says what each one
*gives* the party, and flags where that payoff is missing, weak, or redundant.

## The test I'm applying

You asked that the front of the adventure stay **open-world** (explore grounds
and house, hit encounters in any order, no plot breakage) up to a **culminating
event that drives Douglas to flee to the old church**, after which the party
must **find him and face the finale** — and that the exploration before that
should yield **practically useful clues *and* advantages**, not busywork.

So every Act-1 moment is graded on one question:

> After this encounter, is the party either **materially stronger** for the
> church (a weapon, a ward, a supply, an ally, a usable timing window, a
> win-condition they can now execute) **or pointed at something that will make
> them stronger**?

Verdict legend used throughout:

- ✅ **Direct benefit** — hands over a concrete advantage usable at the church.
- 🔗 **Stepping-stone** — no payoff itself, but unlocks/aims a beneficial follow-up. (You said these are fine.)
- 🔁 **Corroboration** — only re-confirms something already known. Fine in small doses; a problem if that's *all* a site does.
- ⚠️ **Dead-end / flavour-only** — costs table time, returns "understanding" with no lever. These are the ones to fix or cut.

## 1. Story skeleton (the spine)

The mystery, stripped to backstory + four acts. (The source tells this twice and
slightly differently — see §5 for the contradictions. This is the *intended*
spine.)

**Backstory (already true when play begins):**

- A pre-colonial threshold lies under the **lake**; a Merriwether ancestor sealed
  it with a carved **ward-stone**, hidden in a submerged alcove. The old **church
  (St Crispin's)** sits on the *paired* site — the "door," kept shut as long as
  the stone stays in the lake.
- **Sarah Merriwether** drowned in the lake in 1919 investigating the anomaly.
  Her father **Rupert**, consumed by guilt, has been researching how to reach her.
- Rupert shared that research with houseguest **Douglas Kimball** (ex–science
  teacher, occult researcher).
- **Bertrand** (son), also guilt-ridden, performed an amateur ritual at the lake
  Thursday night — weakening the seal and frightening himself badly.
- **Douglas**, already "touched" through the research, communed with the ward-stone
  at the lake through a ritual and became "captured" by the entity held back by the ward-stone. He was able to retrieve the ward-stone and has installed it at the church in a **reversed** configuration, turning himself into the **bridge** for an entity called **the Watcher**.
  Soon it will try to use Douglas to cross over into this world again.

**Act 1 — Arrival & open investigation** *(Fri evening → Sat day)*
The sandbox. Mingling, breakfast intros, then free exploration of house and
grounds. This is where advantages are earned. Fully order-independent.

**Act 2 — The Saturday seance (the hinge / culmination)**
Ophelia's seance turns real, names the threat (church, buried anchor, Watcher,
"Douglas is the bridge"), and **exposes Douglas, who flees**. This is the single
scripted turn — the point of no return. *This is the culminating event you want.*

**Act 3 — The chase & church finale**
Find Douglas at St Crispin's; confront him mid-transformation and the partially
manifested Watcher; resolve (destroy stone / return stone to lake / coerce / bind
/ fight, with Sarah's intervention as a safety net).

**Act 4 — Aftermath** — Douglas's fate, the family, rewards.

> **One recommended structural change up front.** The source splits this across
> two play-sessions with a **full extra investigation day (Sunday)** wedged
> between the seance and the church. That Sunday largely *re-offers the same
> investigation paths and benefits* (library, Bertrand, Rupert, staff, Douglas's
> room all appear twice), and it forces Douglas — who dramatically fled at the
> seance — to reappear at Sunday breakfast. Both things blunt your culmination.
> See §5 and §8. The cleanest spine is **two long sandbox days leading up to → seance → then optional investigation of Douglas' room → scary trek to the church → encounter finale**,
> with the seance as the hinge.

## 2. The open-world map: what's free, what's the hinge, the clock

| | Design intent | How to keep it from breaking |
|---|---|---|
| **Act 1 sites** | Any order, any subset, no gating between them | None should *require* another first. Each must stand alone (some currently assume you visited a different site — see notes). |
| **The hinge** | The seance (Act 2) | This is the **only** beat that must happen on a schedule. Everything before is optional; everything after is a chase. |
| **The clock** | Soft before the seance, hard after | Before the seance: **no time pressure** — let the sandbox breathe. The seance **starts the clock**: the Watcher crosses at the next activation (the 10 pm pulse / dawn), giving a bounded window that justifies prep and forces the finale. Today the script keeps shouting "tonight!" while handing the party ~36 idle hours; that mixed signal is the sequencing problem. |

**Headline principle to protect the incentives:** the **seance delivers PLOT,
investigation delivers POWER.** The seance guarantees every table knows *what*
and *where* (so the story can't hard-stall). Investigation is what makes the
finale survivable — wards, allies, weapons, timing, win-conditions. Keep that
line bright: **no investigation beat should pay out only plot the seance already
gives for free.** Where one does, it's flagged 🔁 below and needs an advantage
attached.

## 5. Plot-logic bugs that hurt sequencing & incentives

1. **Two different "anchor objects." (Most important.)** The front half and
   Rupert's office say the portal's anchor is the **grimoire buried under the
   altar** → *destroy the buried book to close the portal* (and the seance's
   Sarah literally says "the buried book… destroy it"). But the Session-2 mystery
   and the entire finale make the **ward-stone** the key (destroy it or return it
   to the lake); the grimoire all but vanishes at the church. A party that
   researched "destroy the buried book" arrives to find the answer is a stone
   nobody mentioned. **Fix:** pick one model. Cleanest — the **ward-stone is the
   key** (return = reseal, destroy = collapse); the **grimoire is the
   instructions** (it tells you *how*, and is where the return-to-lake method is
   learned). Rewrite Rupert's office, the library, and Sarah's seance line to say
   "the book tells you how," not "destroy the book."

2. **Douglas flees at the seance, then reappears at Sunday breakfast** ("present
   but wrong"). This directly undercuts the culmination you want. **Fix:** after
   the seance, Douglas is **gone for good** (to the church). If you want a
   "present but wrong" deterioration beat, place it *before* the seance (Saturday
   daytime), not after.

3. **A redundant second sandbox (Sunday).** Session 2's morning re-runs library,
   Bertrand, Rupert, staff, and Douglas's-room investigations the party could
   already do Saturday — same benefits, second helping. It dilutes urgency and
   doubles content. **Fix:** either compress to one day (§1), or make Sunday
   *new* (consequences, NPCs turning, estate decaying, a hard clock) rather than
   a repeat.

4. **Urgency vs. open-world whiplash.** "The entity crosses tonight" is repeated
   while the structure grants ~36 leisurely hours. **Fix:** the soft-then-hard
   clock in §2 — no pressure before the seance, bounded countdown after.

5. **Duplicate benefit sources not reconciled.** Protective symbols come from
   *both* library research (A2) *and* the "Geometries of Protection" marked book;
   creature weaknesses and portal mechanics likewise appear in both halves.
   Harmless individually, but it's why the doc feels twice as long as the story.
   **Fix:** one canonical source per benefit, with alternate paths noted as
   "also learnable via…".

1. **Cut** the swimming pool from Path C (C3); fold any value into the lake.
2. **Cut** the fountain ear-pressure and medieval-cherub beats (C2) — keep only the **10 pm bird-death timing**, and make it actionable.
3. **Convert** C1's "the water is wrong" understanding into the **alcove-socket** discovery (return-stone plan) + **collect lake water** action.
4. **Trim** B3's "always a threshold point" lore (or merge into the paired-sites payoff).
5. **Attach a lever** to the two corroboration-only beats: give **E2** gear (lanterns/rope/crowbar), give **F2** the alternate Bertrand-ally unlock.

## 8. Prioritized recommendations

1. **Make the seance the single hinge.** Douglas flees and *stays* gone; the
   clock goes hard only after. Remove or relocate the Sunday re-sandbox. *(Fixes
   your sequencing concern directly.)*
2. **Resolve the anchor contradiction** (§5 #1): ward-stone = key, grimoire =
   instructions. Reconcile Sarah's "destroy the buried book" line accordingly.
3. **Rehabilitate Path C** (§7 #1–3): turn the water cluster from the adventure's
   dead-end zone into the source of the **lake-water weapon** and the
   **return-stone win-condition**.
4. **Convert two roleplay/corroboration beats into advantages** (A7 → finale
   humanity bonus; E2 → gear), so social and thorough play both pay out.
5. **Consolidate duplicated benefit sources** (§5 #5) to one canonical entry
   each — this alone will shrink the script and clarify the open-world map.
6. **Add a soft clock + a prep checklist** so players can *see* the advantages
   they've banked before the finale.
