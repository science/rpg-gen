# The Paper Chase

*A scenario for Call of Cthulhu, 7th edition. New England, July 1924. One Keeper, 3–5 investigators, two sessions of play.*

A reclusive millionaire gathers skeptics and seekers at his country estate to "explore the occult sciences." Beneath the manicured grounds an old door is being pried open, a grieving man is becoming something else, and the only people who notice are the guests who came for a parlor game. This is a sandbox of quiet dread that tightens, over two days, into a single desperate night.

---

## Keeper's Overview — The Truth

Read this once; the players should learn it in fragments.

Generations ago a Merriwether ancestor, **Jeremiah**, settled this land because it was *thin* — a place where the membrane between our world and somewhere colder wears through. The weak point sits beneath the **lake**. Jeremiah's people sealed it with a carved basalt **ward-stone**, sunk in a submerged stone alcove fifty yards offshore, and built the little **church of St Crispin** on the *paired* site in the woods — the "door," which stays shut as long as the stone stays in the water. The family kept the arrangement as a private superstition, tending the church, warning the children away from it.

In **1919**, Rupert's daughter **Sarah Merriwether** drowned in the lake while investigating the anomaly her father had dismissed. Her death broke **Rupert**. For five years he has poured his fortune into one secret project: to reopen the threshold and speak to her again. He researched the family papers, corresponded with occult scholars, and — fatally — shared what he learned with a houseguest who seemed to *understand*: **Douglas Kimball**, a quiet former schoolteacher and lifelong student of "thin places."

Douglas understood too well. Drawn to the lake by Rupert's notes, he performed his own ritual there and was **taken** — not possessed exactly, but *enlisted*. The thing behind the stone, which old papers call **the Watcher**, found in Douglas a man already attuned to thresholds and made him its instrument. Over the past week, in a fugue he only half remembers, Douglas **lifted the ward-stone from the lake**, carried it to St Crispin's, and set it on the altar in a *reversed* configuration. The seal at the lake is broken; the door in the woods is opening. Douglas is becoming the **bridge** the Watcher needs to cross — a little less human every night.

It will finish **this weekend**. The séance the guests have come to enjoy is the moment the Watcher reaches all the way through.

**The two endings the party can engineer:**

- **Reseal (best):** carry the ward-stone back to its alcove in the lake. The door shuts for another century; Douglas, severed from the crossing while still partly human, can be saved.
- **Collapse:** destroy the ward-stone (smash it, or topple the altar onto it). The portal tears closed and strands the Watcher on the far side — violent, and Douglas may be dragged through unless pulled clear.

**The anchor, made consistent (important — the source contradicts itself here):** the **ward-stone is the key**. The **grimoire** wrapped in oilskin and buried beneath the church altar is *not* a thing to destroy — it is the **instructions**, the family's record of how the seal works and how to restore it. Anywhere a clue says "destroy the buried book," it should instead read "**the buried book tells you how**." Keep this straight and the mystery stays clean.

## Running This Adventure

**The shape.** Friday and Saturday are an **open sandbox**: the investigators wander the house and grounds, meet people, and dig. Encounters can happen in almost any order. The one fixed beat is the **Saturday-evening séance**, the hinge of the whole scenario. After it, the gloves come off and the adventure becomes a single forced march to the church that night.

**Séance delivers plot, investigation delivers power.** The séance guarantees that *every* table — even one that spent two days at the dinner table — walks away knowing the essentials: the threat is at the church, it involves a stone and a buried book, an entity called the Watcher is crossing, and Douglas is its bridge. That keeps the story from stalling. But the *advantages* that make the finale survivable — weapons, wards, salt, lake-water, allies, the timing window — come **only** from investigation. Lean on this whenever players ask "do we have to dig?" They don't have to. They'll just face the Watcher with their bare hands if they don't.

**The clock: soft, then hard.** Before the séance, there is *no* time pressure — let the sandbox breathe, let players chase what interests them. The instant Douglas flees the séance, a hard countdown starts: the crossing culminates **tonight**. See *The Forced March* (below) for the dilemma this creates.

**Benefits are a currency.** Most scenes hand the party a concrete, finale-usable thing. The text flags these as **BENEFIT →**. Appendix C is a one-page tracker; tick benefits off as they're earned so you can scale the finale's danger to how prepared the party is. A loaded party should find the church hard but winnable; an unprepared one should find it terrifying.

**Dread, used sparingly.** This scenario deliberately seeds **one or two** unsettling "is it a trick, a haunting, or something *worse*?" moments into the calm — chiefly the **fountain** (Saturday) and **Sarah's memorial** (anytime). Don't overuse them. Two good scares across two days is plenty; more and the slow build collapses into a haunted-house ride.

**Conventions in this text:**

> Indented quotes are **read-aloud** text — describe in your own words if you prefer.

Plain paragraphs after a heading are Keeper guidance. Skill checks are written **Skill (difficulty):** with the result spelled out; "difficulty" is omitted for a Regular roll. **BENEFIT →** marks a banked advantage. Sanity losses are written loss/loss (e.g. **0/1D4**).

## Dramatis Personae

**The Host's Family**

- **Rupert Merriwether, 67** — host, widower's grief twice over. Courtly, exhausted, brilliant, and quietly coming apart. He opened this door and cannot close it; he is terrified of what he's done and desperate for absolution. *Knows the most. Confesses if pressed with compassion or evidence.*
- **Agnes Merriwether, 63** — Rupert's wife, Sarah's mother. Watchful, practical, frightened *for* her husband rather than *of* anything. She doesn't know the occult particulars but knows Rupert hasn't slept properly in years. Will protect and reward kindness to her family.
- **Bertrand Merriwether, 33** — the surviving child. He and Sarah argued the day she drowned; he has carried it like a stone ever since. **Friday night he drives to the lake to attempt his own clumsy ritual to reach her** — and comes back shaking, muddy, and certain something watched him from the water. Guilt-sick and on the edge of confession. *The most readily-won ally: Occult 45%, can draw a ward, knows the woods.*

**The Guests**

- **Douglas Kimball, 52** — former high-school science teacher, obsessive amateur occultist. Reads constantly; speaks rarely, and only with sudden fervor, about "crossing over." **He is the antagonist and the victim** — the Watcher's bridge, hollowing out a little more each night, unaware by day of what he does by dark. He can still be saved, for now.
- **Ophelia Ashcroft, 44** — medium and psychic, hired by Rupert six months ago. A practiced showwoman who is, to her own growing horror, *genuinely* sensitive — and this house is screaming at her. *A strong potential ally if treated with respect rather than as a fraud.*
- **The investigators** — guests of the **Society for the Exploration of the Unexplained (SEU)**, invited through Rupert directly or via mutual acquaintances, here for five summer weeks of seances, discussion, and the run of a magnificent private library. Skeptics and believers both are welcome.

**The Staff** *(below stairs; full roster in the source if you want names)*

The household runs on roughly twenty servants under **Butler Taylor Rickards**, **Head Maid Mrs Smouth**, **Cook Mrs Kelkin**, **Groundskeeper Mr Goshel**, and **Chauffeur Oscar Meadow** with his mechanic **Potter Farms**. They are loyal, underpaid, and uneasy: "things haven't been right since Miss Sarah passed." Two details matter for play — **the house is cleaned top to bottom and locked down at 8 p.m.** sharp each night, and the staff have been buying **salt in absurd quantities** on Mrs Kelkin's nervous insistence.

## The Estate

A 1920s pleasure-palace of new money, dropped into old New England woods. Describe it as intoxicating and faintly oppressive — too much marble, too much quiet.

**House — open to guests**
- **The Library** — two connected rooms, floor-to-ceiling, with locked glass cases of genuinely rare occult texts. The research hub. *(See Saturday §The Library.)*
- **The Great Dining Hall** — Friday's dinner and Saturday's séance happen here.
- **The Kitchen & Servants' Hall** — welcoming only if you earn it. *(Charm/Persuade.)*

**House — locked or watched** *(snooping has consequences: a stern warning, then expulsion if repeated)*
- **Rupert's Study** — always locked. The richest single trove of intel.
- **The Guest Wing** — Douglas's, Bertrand's, and Ophelia's rooms. Usually locked.
- **The Butler's Office** — keys, the household's daily logbook.

**Grounds — open and near**
- **The Lake, Dock & Boathouse** — three skiffs; unnaturally still water; the drowned heart of the mystery.
- **The Fountain & Formal Garden** — a baroque fountain with a brooding stone cherub. *(A dread beat lives here.)*
- **The Motor Garage** — Oscar and Potter's domain. *(Skill-gated welcome.)*
- **Sarah's Memorial** — a small, recently-built stone shrine near the **stables**, with her name and a sculpted relief of her face. The family lays flowers here. *(A dread beat lives here.)*
- **The Summer House, Gazebo, Pool & Hedge Maze** — atmosphere. Lovely, empty, and — deliberately — *not* hiding required clues. Let players relax here; that's its job.

**Grounds — far and gated**
- **St Crispin's Church & graveyard** — a collapsed stone chapel from the early 1700s, **a stiff 45-minute walk** through the north woods past the old stone wall. Sarah is buried in its tiny family plot. This is the finale site; reaching it by day is a real expedition (see Saturday §The Far Church), and most parties will first see it at night.

# ACT ONE — FRIDAY

## Arrival (10 a.m.)

> The motor-drive winds a half-mile through chestnut woods before the trees release you onto a great green lawn, and the house — white, columned, absurd — rises out of it like a wedding cake left in the sun. A butler with a face like a closed door hands you down from the car. "Mr Merriwether is at his correspondence," he says, "but the grounds are entirely yours. Dinner is at seven. The house retires at eight." Behind him a footman is already lifting your cases. Somewhere past the topiary, water is running.

Keeper: keep arrival light and warm. The point is to establish the sandbox and let players feel the scale and the money. Rupert is "indisposed" until dinner (he is, in fact, in the church). Other guests drift in across the morning. Give each investigator a moment to be *seen* — a servant who recognizes a name, a fellow guest who's read their monograph.

**Optional, one per investigator:** drop a single free hook so even a purely social player leaves Act One with a thread to pull. Examples: a maid mentions the master "walks the grounds at night now"; a guest remarks the lake "gives them the shivers, and they couldn't say why"; Bertrand, passing, flinches at the word *Sarah*.

## Friday Afternoon — Sandbox 1 (Orientation)

This first window is **deliberately lighter** than Saturday. The locked rooms are locked, Rupert is absent, and the staff are busy and formal. What's available now is the *open* grounds and the *first read* on people and places. Treat anything investigators do here as laying groundwork they'll cash in Saturday.

What's live this afternoon:
- **The Library** is open and browseable (the Luger is findable now; deep research is better saved for Saturday when there's time — but don't forbid it).
- **The Lake, Fountain, Garden, Summer House, Maze** are all walkable. The lake's *wrongness* is already apparent; the fountain dread beat *can* fire now but plays better Saturday once tension has built (your call).
- **Sarah's Memorial** by the stables can be found now — a quiet, sad place. The dread beat there is gentle enough to use early as a first chill.
- **The Garage** and **Kitchen** require a social roll to be made welcome; the staff are stiffer today than they'll be tomorrow once the guests have "settled in."

Keeper guidance: resist front-loading revelations. Let the afternoon be about texture — the unnatural quiet of the lake, the salt deliveries glimpsed at the kitchen door, the fresh flowers at the memorial. Plant questions; pay them off Saturday.

## The Formal Dinner (7 p.m.) — the lore engine

The single most important *social* scene in the scenario. Everyone is present; this is where the world, the family, and the stakes get explained, and where the party makes the friends (or enemies) who matter at the finale.

> The dining hall could seat forty; tonight it seats a dozen, and the candlelight gives up long before it reaches the ceiling. Rupert Merriwether stands to greet you — tall, silver, gracious, with the hollowed eyes of a man who hasn't slept since the war. "My friends. Skeptics, believers, and the honestly undecided — you are all equally welcome. For five weeks this house is a laboratory of the impossible. We will hold seances. We will argue. We will read." He smiles. "And tomorrow night, Miss Ashcroft will attempt to part the veil for us. I would be grateful if you came to it with open minds — or at least, open eyes."

Work the table as a set of small scenes. The lore lands through conversation, not lecture:

- **Rupert** holds forth on "the Occult Sciences," the SEU, and his conviction that death is "a threshold, not a wall." **Psychology** reads desperate hope under the showmanship. If anyone mentions the lake or the old church, he goes briefly still and changes the subject. He will *not* discuss Sarah at the table.
- **Agnes** watches Rupert the whole meal. To a sympathetic neighbor she murmurs that she wishes he'd "let the dead be dead," and that he "walks half the night now."
- **Douglas Kimball** eats in silence until thresholds come up — then he ignites, talking too fast and too brightly about "thin places" and "crossing over as a discipline, not a séance trick." **Psychology:** beneath the fervor he is *frightened* and exhausted. **BENEFIT → Douglas rapport:** an investigator who engages him warmly (Charm, or simply shared fascination) earns his trust; he confides he's been having "extraordinary dreams — of a stone corridor, and water that falls *upward*." A rapport built here makes the finale's appeal-to-his-humanity *far* easier (see The Church, Phase 1).
- **Ophelia Ashcroft** is all professional poise — until pressed. Treat her as a fraud and she ices over (and is harder to recruit later). Treat her with respect and **BENEFIT → Ophelia's regard:** she admits, quietly, that she's "had no peace since arriving — this house is *loud*," and that she'd value an ally with sense. This goodwill becomes her active help at the séance and the church.
- **Bertrand** drinks too much and says too little, flinching whenever his sister or the grounds come up. He is visibly screwing up courage for something. **Psychology:** guilt, not menace. A kind word now is the first deposit toward winning him as an ally tomorrow.

Keeper: by the end of dinner every table should know — *this family lost a daughter to the lake; the host believes the dead can be reached; tomorrow there's a séance; and the old church in the woods is somewhere nobody wants to talk about.* That's the plot floor for Act One. Everything sharper is earned by digging.

## Friday Night — the house sleeps, Bertrand does not

After dinner the household winds down. Establish, plainly, the clue that pays off tomorrow:

> At eight o'clock the house transforms. Maids sweep through every corridor; the footmen check the locks; Mrs Smouth runs a cloth along the banisters herself. By half-past, the public rooms are spotless and dim, the doors to the study and the guest wing are locked, and the staff have vanished below stairs. The estate is *put to bed*.

Keeper: make sure the players clock the **8 p.m. lockdown and deep-clean**. Don't explain why it matters — just establish it as the house's rhythm. (Tomorrow, mud that appears *after* the cleaning becomes a damning timestamp.)

**Bertrand's lake trip** happens around midnight whether or not anyone sees it. Wracked by guilt and half-believing his father's talk, Bertrand takes the Rolls down the service lane to the lake and attempts a fumbling ritual from Rupert's notes — calling for Sarah. The lake answers wrongly. He flees in a panic, clips a tree with the fender, and returns near dawn muddy to the knees and white as paper.

**Optional near-miss (a soft dread beat).** If any investigators are out on the grounds late — stargazing, sneaking, sleepless — let them catch it at a distance:

> Down at the dock, a single electric torch jerks and stutters. A man's voice — low, pleading, you can't make out the words. Then the torch drops. The water, which has lain mirror-flat all evening, *humps* upward in a slow black swell, as though something vast had turned over just beneath the surface. The man bolts for the car. An engine screams. By the time you reach the dock there is nothing but wet planking and three motionless skiffs — and the smell of stagnant water, where this afternoon there was none.

**Listen/Spot Hidden** to identify the fleeing figure as Bertrand; **Track** at the dock at this hour finds fresh boot-prints and grey lake-clay. This is **tension, not combat** — the water does nothing but *suggest*. SAN **0/1** for the swell, if anyone watched it closely.

Whether or not it's witnessed, it leaves evidence: a dented Rolls, mud where mud shouldn't be, and a man at breakfast who looks like he's seen the bottom of the world.

# ACT TWO — SATURDAY

## Breakfast (8 a.m.) — direction without spoon-feeding

> Breakfast is laid in the morning room, all silver and sunlight, and entirely failing to lift the mood. Rupert pushes food around his plate and stares at nothing. Bertrand's hands shake so badly his cup chatters in its saucer; there's a raw scrape on the back of one wrist. Agnes watches them both. And Mrs Smouth, supervising from the doorway, is *furious* about something — her eyes keep snapping to the foot of the main staircase.

This scene aims players at threads without naming them. Things to be noticed:

- **Spot Hidden (the staircase):** a smear of **grey clay mud** dried on the third step and the hall runner — and the head maid's quiet rage about it. **Library Use/Know or simple logic:** *the house was spotless at 8 p.m.; this mud arrived in the night.* Whoever tracked it in was out on the grounds after lockdown. **BENEFIT → the "after-8" heuristic:** from here on, anything muddy, damp, or disturbed is a timestamp pointing at nocturnal activity.
- **Psychology (Bertrand):** terror and guilt, barely contained. If approached gently he'll whisper a request: *"Can we speak? Privately. After. I have to tell someone what I've done."* (Leads to his confession — see The Garage / Bertrand.)
- **Psychology (Rupert):** he is somewhere far away and afraid. Pressed (Hard Persuade or Intimidate) he allows only: *"I may have begun something I cannot end. Forgive me — not at the table."*
- **Ophelia** arrives last, having slept badly: *"I dreamt of a drowned corridor. Something at the end of it was awake, and glad I'd come."* If she's an ally, she shares this freely; if alienated, she keeps to herself.
- **Douglas's empty chair.** Douglas does not come to breakfast. ("Reading," a footman offers. He is asleep, recovering from the night.) His absence is itself a thread.

Then Rupert reminds everyone, with brittle brightness, that the séance is tonight after dinner — and the day is theirs.

## The Investigation — Sandbox 2 (the main event)

This is the heart of the scenario: an open day in which the party can visit locations in any order and accumulate the clues and advantages they'll need. Below, each site lists **access · what's there · the rolls · the benefits**. Nothing here gates anything else; every site stands alone. Two **dread beats** (Fountain, Memorial) are woven in — use one or both, not the kitchen sink.

> **Keeper shortcut:** if a table is drifting, the strongest single thread is *the mud → the lake → the empty alcove → the church*. The richest single room is **Rupert's study**. The most valuable single ally is **Bertrand**. Steer toward those if needed; let players find the rest.

### The Library

> Two long rooms, joined by an arch, lined to the ceiling in leather and gilt. A rolling ladder. A green-shaded desk. And, behind locked glass, a shelf of books that have no business in a gentleman's collection — titles in Latin, in Arabic, in no alphabet at all. One section, on "dimensional theory," is so heavily used the spines are cracked white.

**Browsing (no roll).** The dimensional-theory shelf shows two distinct hands of marginalia — one elegant and old, one cramped and recent. **Spot Hidden:** wrapped in oilcloth at the back of the desk's bottom drawer, a **German Luger pistol, loaded, five rounds**. Someone hid it here, in easy reach, recently. **BENEFIT → Weapon (Luger, 5 rounds).** *(Later, Handwriting/Library Use can match the elegant hand to Rupert's study journals and the cramped one to Douglas — confirming the two men were collaborators.)*

**Research (Library Use, ~1 hour each).** Pick a topic; success surfaces a usable text. *Consolidate the source's two libraries into this one canonical shelf.*

- **"Protective Symbols & Wards"** → *Geometries of Protection.* Teaches Elder Signs, containment circles, and the banishment glyph. **BENEFIT → Wards.** *(Full mechanics in Appendix A. Note: reading it costs 1 SAN — the angles are subtly wrong.)* **Cross-reference:** the text specifies that **salt** is the ideal medium, making every ward easier to draw — which is why the cook's salt matters.
- **"Portals & Thresholds"** → *Thresholds and Thin Places.* Weak points cluster near water, old stone, and sites of death; they come in **paired sites** — one where force *gathers*, one where it is *sealed*. Closing one requires either destroying its **anchor** or performing a banishment at a threshold hour (dawn/dusk). **BENEFIT → Portal mechanics** (and the dawn/dusk note feeds the finale's timing dilemma).
- **"Creatures Beyond"** (Hard Library Use) → a monograph on liminal entities, "Watchers," that cannot hold a shape in **bright light** and are most vulnerable mid-transition. A marginal note, in the old elegant hand, reads only: *"It fears the lake. Why the lake?"* **BENEFIT → Creature weaknesses (light; and a deliberately vague hook toward lake-water).** Keep the lake clue *inferential* — let the players reason out *that water from the sealed site is poison to the thing*; don't hand it to them.
- **"The Merriwether Family"** → a privately-printed family history. **Jeremiah Merriwether** (1680s) "bound what lurked beneath the waters" with "a ward-stone carved by those who came before." Sarah, 1919, "lost to the lake." **BENEFIT → Motive & the ward-stone's existence;** and a lever to confront Rupert ("we know what Jeremiah did — what have *you* done?").

**Deep research (3+ hrs total, Hard Library Use).** Buried in the locked case: ***Rituals of Binding and Dismissal.*** *(Per your edit, the Latin gate is dropped — translation comes with the reading time, no language roll.)* **BENEFIT → The Binding Ritual:** with a salt circle, three chanters, and ~15 minutes (5 if Ophelia leads), an entity can be *trapped* — buying the party time and options at the church.

**The hidden books (Spot Hidden, or noticing the mis-shelving).** A handful of the most useful protective texts have been **deliberately hidden** — shoved behind others, re-shelved in the wrong sections. Whoever did it knew exactly which books threatened the plan. **BENEFIT → Inference:** *the enemy fears these books — therefore they're the right ones.* (It was night-Douglas, on the Watcher's prompting; he won't remember.)

**Douglas in the library (afternoon).** If he's not in his room, Douglas is here, devouring the dimensional shelf. A rapport from dinner (or a fresh Charm) makes him open and human and sad; he speaks of the church "calling" to him in dreams and doesn't understand why. **BENEFIT → deepened rapport** (compounds the finale humanity-appeal). **Psychology:** he is being *hollowed out* — and has no idea.

### The Lake, Dock & Boathouse

> The lake should be loud with July — frogs, insects, the slap of fish. It is silent. The water lies flat and grey as a coin, and nothing moves on it or in it. The air smells faintly of stagnant ponds, though this water is spring-fed and clear.

**Natural World or Science:** the ecosystem is *dead* — no insects, no fish, no algae bloom in high summer. This is not pollution; it's *absence*, as if life declines to be here. **(Understanding — but it points at the lake as the wound.)**

**Spot Hidden (dock):** one skiff's floorboards are **damp**, though no one has rowed today; grey clay on the gunwale. Someone took a boat out in the night.

**Taking a skiff out** (the boathouse has three): about fifty yards offshore, **Spot Hidden** catches a squared shadow under the surface — worked stone, far older than the estate.

- **Swim/Dive:** a sunken foundation, slick with black algae, pre-colonial. Set into it is a **stone alcove** — and the alcove is **empty**, its lip freshly scarred where something forearm-sized was levered out. **Occult/Archaeology:** this is the *original* sealed site; whatever filled this socket was **pried loose within the last week**. **BENEFIT → The reseal plan (the single most important clue for the best ending):** *something was removed from here that belongs here.* When the party later finds the ward-stone on the church altar, they'll know where it goes.
- **Touching the algae:** SAN **0/1** — a wave of wrongness, of being *noticed* from below.
- **Filling a flask:** an investigator who thinks to collect **lake water** has it for the finale. Encourage this if anyone's pieced together the "it fears the lake" hook. **BENEFIT → Lake-water (a weapon against the Watcher).**

### The Fountain & Garden — *dread beat (keystone)*

The baroque fountain is the scenario's sanctioned scare *with a payoff*. Run it Saturday, when tension has built.

> The formal garden is the prettiest spot on the grounds and the birds avoid it. Two of them lie dead on the fountain's rim — perfect, unmarked, as though their hearts simply stopped. The centerpiece is a weathered stone cherub, but the sculptor gave it a strange, grave, almost grieving face, and it leans out over a basin of water so still it looks solid.

Let an investigator lean in to look — at the dead birds, at their reflection, at a glint on the basin floor (a few coins, a child's lost ring — bait for curiosity).

> Your reflection holds for a moment. Then it *thickens*. The water doesn't ripple so much as *flex*, and something the colour of a drowned thing uncoils up out of a basin that is barely two feet deep — a slick grey limb, far too long, that lashes around your forearm and **pulls**.

**This is brief and genuinely dangerous.** Resolve fast:

- The grabbed investigator makes an opposed **STR/Dodge** to avoid being yanked toward the water (treat the limb as STR 60). A second character can grapple, strike, or haul them back.
- Anyone striking the limb finds it **rubbery and cold**; ordinary blows barely tell. A lantern or a struck match makes it *flinch* (foreshadowing the light weakness).
- The limb retreats after 1 round regardless — into water that is, when anyone dares look, once more two feet deep and empty. SAN **1/1D4**.

**BENEFIT → Two finale-relevant lessons, paid for in fear:**
1. **The Watcher can reach through any still water on the grounds** — the fountain, the pool, a full bath. (Raise the players' guard; suggest the lake is its true conduit.)
2. **Heavy leather turns its grip.** The investigator's coat-sleeve (or a glove, or a gardener's apron) is where the limb *failed* to hold — it slid off the thick hide. A clever party infers that **a leather apron or heavy coat is real armor against its tentacles** at the church. *(If no one notices, the groundskeeper or Bertrand can voice it: "Funny — it couldn't keep hold of the leather.")* **BENEFIT → Leather defense.**

**The dead birds** still carry their quieter clue: **Natural World** dates the deaths to roughly the same hour each night; a return visit or a word with the groundskeeper pins it to **~10 p.m.**, beginning three nights ago. **BENEFIT → Timing (something happens nightly at 10).**

*(The pool, gazebo, summer house and maze remain pure atmosphere — explicitly no required clues. If players search them raw, reward the effort with a coin of mood, not a plot beat.)*

### Sarah's Memorial (by the stables) — *dread beat (secondary)*

> Behind the stables, where the family can grieve unseen, someone has built a little shrine of pale stone — newer than everything around it. SARAH MERRIWETHER, 1894–1919. Below the name, carved in shallow relief, her face: a young woman, half-smiling, rendered with more love than skill. Fresh-cut flowers lie at its foot. The horses, twenty feet away, will not settle.

A place of genuine grief — and a soft haunting. Let players have a quiet beat here (Rupert comes most mornings; a thoughtful investigator might wait and catch him at his most vulnerable and confessable). Then, once, gently:

> For just a moment, the carved eyes are *wet* — two thin runnels of water tracking down the stone face, smelling of the lake — and a voice you feel more than hear breathes across the back of your neck: *"He didn't listen. Don't go in the water."* Then it's only stone, and dry, and the flowers, and the uneasy horses.

SAN **0/1D3**. **BENEFIT → Sarah is present and on your side.** This foreshadows her intervention at the finale and makes it *earned*: a party that grieves honestly here is one she'll reach for when they're losing. It also quietly reinforces the lake as the danger. *(If you want a sharper scare for a brave party, the relief's hand can seem to grasp at a sleeve — startling, harmless.)*

### The Garage & Bertrand

**Access:** the garage welcomes you with **Charm, Fast Talk, or Mechanical Repair** (talk shop with Potter).

> Oil, chrome, and the smell of petrol. Three magnificent cars — and the Rolls, nose-in, with a fresh crease in its front fender that someone has tried to buff out.

**Potter (mechanic), loosened up:** *"Mr Bertrand took the Rolls out Thursday — no, Friday night, late. Brought her back before dawn looking like he'd driven her through a bog. Mud to the axles, and him shaking like a leaf. He's not been himself."*

**Spot Hidden:** grey clay on the undercarriage, reeking of stagnant water — *not* local soil. **Science/Natural World:** it matches the lake bottom. **BENEFIT → Evidence: Bertrand went to the lake, at night, and something there terrified him.**

**Bertrand's confession.** Whether the party corners him on this evidence or honors his breakfast plea, Bertrand breaks — and *how they treat him decides whether he becomes an ally.*

> "Sarah and I quarreled the day she drowned. Terrible things. I've carried it for five years. When Father started his — his *research* — I thought, God help me, that I might reach her. Apologize. So Friday night I took his notes to the lake and I... I *tried.*" He's shaking. "The water *moved.* There was something under it — not Sarah. Old. *Watching.* I ran. And when I got back, Douglas was at his window. Awake. He *saw* me. The next night he went down to the woods himself, and he came back carrying something."

**Persuade/Charm (compassion, not blame):** he tells the rest — his father's warning that the church is "a door that should stay closed," his certainty that Douglas is trying to open it, that he can guide the party there through the woods. **BENEFIT → Sequence of events confirmed; the church named as the site.** **BENEFIT → Ally: Bertrand** — if treated with kindness he insists on coming to make it right (Occult 45%, can draw a ward correctly, knows the woods path in the dark). *Treated with contempt, he collapses into useless guilt and stays home.*

### The Kitchen & Staff

**Access:** **Charm or Persuade** to be made welcome past the threshold; Mrs Kelkin is stern but kind underneath.

**What the staff give up,** once they trust you:
- *"Nothing's been right since Miss Sarah passed."* The master doesn't sleep; there are cold spots and self-opening doors in the east wing; **Douglas has been seen from the windows walking to the old church at night, carrying things, and coming back "wrong — taller, somehow."*
- **Spot Hidden / the larder:** an absurd quantity of **salt** — a fifty-pound delivery this week alone, on Mrs Kelkin's anxious orders ("Mother always said salt keeps the bad out"). A bag goes missing easily. **BENEFIT → Salt** (the medium that makes every ward reliable — tie this to *Geometries of Protection* in the library).
- **The groundskeeper's shed** yields the practical kit of any country estate: **lanterns and lamp-oil, coils of rope, a crowbar, an axe.** **BENEFIT → Gear** (light to repel the Watcher; rope to restrain Douglas; crowbar to topple the altar or smash the stone).

### The Locked Rooms (high risk, high reward)

**Access:** **Locksmith**, a lifted key, or force — with consequences. Caught snooping earns a furious warning; a second offense, expulsion (and the loss of easy access to everything else). Reward boldness; punish carelessness.

**Rupert's Study** *(the single richest room)*

> A scholar's lair gone obsessive: maps pinned over maps, correspondence in drifts, and a locked drawer that gives to a Locksmith roll.

- Letters proving he **hired Ophelia** six months ago, and begged a dozen scholars for ways "to contact the dead."
- His **journal**, in the elegant hand from the library: *"Sarah's death was no accident — she was investigating the lake I'd dismissed. I failed her." … "I've made contact. Something answers. But it is not Sarah." … "I must close what I have opened, and I do not know how."*
- **Ritual notes** matching the configuration at the church, with the crucial line: *"The seal is the stone, not the book. Father's grimoire lies beneath the altar in oilskin — it records the binding, the means of restoration. The book is the instructions. The stone is the key."* **BENEFIT → The anchor, made plain:** *return or destroy the stone; the buried book tells you how.* This is the text that resolves the whole mystery — let it land.
- A hand-drawn **ley-line map** marking lake and church as paired "convergence points." **BENEFIT → Tactical map** of the site and the woods route.

**Douglas's Room** *(timing & the save-condition)*

> The bed hasn't been slept in. A suitcase stands packed by the door, as if he meant to flee and couldn't. A journal lies open on the desk.

Douglas's diary, the handwriting degrading entry by entry, tells the week's arc — finding the lake structure, lifting the stone "without deciding to," preparing the church, and the dawning, awful joy of *"I am the bridge. I was always meant to be."* The final, barely legible line: *"Tonight. Then I cross. Then I understand everything."* And earlier, a planner's note in a clearer hand:

> *"The master is strongest from ten until four. I am strongest — most *changed* — from ten until two. After two I am too far gone to think, too much *his.* Before midnight I am still mostly myself."*

**BENEFIT → The timing window & the save-condition:** Douglas is **most human before midnight** and **lost after two**; the Watcher owns the deep night (10–4). To save Douglas, the party must reach him and sever the crossing **while he's still partly human.** That is the engine of the forced march. **Spot Hidden:** grey lake-mud on his boots in the corner, and a folded sketch of the church interior with the altar marked **X**.

**Bertrand's Room** *(alternate ally-unlock)*

For a party that burgles rather than befriends: a guilt-soaked diary ("I tried Father's ritual at the lake — I saw it looking back at me — I've made everything worse") and increasingly distorted sketches of the church. **BENEFIT → The same confirmation as his confession,** and grounds to approach him as an ally even if the social scene was missed — so Bertrand isn't locked behind a single Charm roll.

### The Far Church (optional daytime expedition)

A party can scout St Crispin's by day — but it is **a real undertaking**, not a stroll: a 45-minute walk each way through trackless north woods (Navigate, or Bertrand/the groundskeeper as guide; getting lost burns the afternoon), and the woods grow colder and quieter the nearer you come. Make the *cost* explicit so most tables save the church for the night — and so those who pay it feel rewarded.

If they go (condensing the source's Path B), they can find by daylight what others meet in the dark: the sealed-but-openable door; the disturbed altar floor and the four **stolen library books**; an amateur, **broken** summoning circle in chalk; the trapdoor to a **basement** with pre-Christian carvings and **Douglas's nest and night-journal**; and, in the little graveyard, **Sarah's grave** with a freshly-disturbed patch of earth.

**Daytime benefits (the reward for the long walk):**
- **BENEFIT → Layout & a pre-set ward:** knowing the door and floor plan, the party can **draw an Elder Sign on the doorframe in advance** (Appendix A) — exactly the move the finale's tactics reward — and gains familiarity (a bonus to their first roll inside tonight).
- **BENEFIT → The altar is toppleable** (Mechanical Repair/Archaeology): three people, or one with the crowbar as a lever — the "collapse" win-condition, scouted.
- **BENEFIT → The buried bundle:** digging at the disturbed patch by Sarah's grave turns up Douglas's spare journal and **a clear sketch of the ritual layout** — the party will know exactly how the stone sits before they walk in.

**Risk:** lingering past dusk, or a Hard Spot Hidden in the basement, risks an early brush with night-Douglas (see Appendix B) and the SAN that comes with it. *(If the party skips the church entirely, none of this is lost — it simply surfaces live during tonight's finale.)*

# THE SÉANCE — THE HINGE (Saturday, ~8 p.m.)

After a tense dinner, the guests gather. **This scene delivers the plot floor to everyone and ends the sandbox.** Whatever the party did or didn't find, they leave the table knowing the shape of the thing — and chasing Douglas into the dark.

> The dining hall has been transformed: heavy curtains drawn against the dusk, the long table cleared but for a ring of candles, the chairs pulled close. Ophelia Ashcroft sits at the head, Rupert at her right hand, his knuckles white on the cloth. "Join hands," she says. "Whatever you believe, do not break the circle." The candles bow. "Spirits of this place — we are listening. *Make yourselves known.*"

Run it in three escalating phases. Keep advantages *out* of it — the séance is for plot.

**Phase 1 — Theatrical (ambiguity).** Lights bob at the room's edges; the table trembles. Let players react in character. **"I want to believe" (Occult):** some of these lights are *not* hidden lamps. **"She's a fraud" (Spot Hidden):** you catch a wire, a confederate's foot — and yet not *all* of it. Even skeptics can't explain everything. *(No mechanical reward; this is mood and buy-in.)*

**Phase 2 — Escalation.** Cupboards rattle. The temperature plunges; breath fogs. Ophelia's eyes roll white and she goes rigid. Around the circle, **Psychology** reads the family laid bare: **Douglas** sheened with sweat and *terrified*; **Rupert** leaning in, desperate with hope; **Bertrand** unable to meet anyone's eye. This is the moment the table realizes the parlor game has teeth.

**Phase 3 — Contact (the guaranteed reveal).** Ophelia's voice drops into something that is not hers:

> *"The threshold… is open. Sarah tried to warn him. Her father would not listen. And now it feeds…"*

A pause — then a second voice, younger, a woman's, frightened and kind:

> *"The church — in the woods — the stone on the altar — put it back, or break it — the book beneath tells you how — before the Watcher crosses. Douglas is the bridge. **Douglas is the door.**"*

Ophelia gasps and breaks the trance, genuinely shaken — *"There is something here. Something that does not belong. It has been crossing over, using a tool to reach—"* — and her eyes go to **Douglas.**

> Douglas Kimball is on his feet before he knows it, the chair clattering behind him. For one instant he looks at you with his own eyes — naked, pleading, *human* — and then he turns and runs from the room, out into the dark, toward the woods.

**The plot floor, now in every player's hands:** the threat is at the **church**; there's a **stone** on the altar (put it back / break it) and a **buried book** that explains how; an entity called the **Watcher** is crossing; **Douglas is the bridge**; **Sarah** is trying to help. **BENEFIT → Ally: Ophelia** — drained but resolute, she offers to come: *"Whatever that was, your host cannot face it alone. Neither can you."* (Her presence halves the binding ritual's time and steadies the party's nerve.)

**Douglas does not come back.** This is the point of no return. The hard clock starts now.

# ACT THREE — INTO THE NIGHT

The séance shatters the evening. The household erupts — Agnes weeping, Rupert ashen, servants milling. Cut through the chaos: the party has perhaps an hour before the night's danger peaks, and exactly one thing worth doing before they march.

## The Forced March (the dilemma — state it clearly)

Lay the choice in front of the players, using whatever intel they've earned:

- **Go now (the hard, right choice).** It is full dark. The Watcher owns the hours from 10 to 4, and Douglas is most dangerous before 2 — but he is also **still partly human and savable** before then, and the crossing is **not yet complete**. Reach the altar tonight and they can still close the door *and* save the man.
- **Wait for dawn (the tempting trap).** At the dawn threshold the Watcher is weakest — but Douglas will have **crossed and been lost**, and the crossing itself may **finish before sunrise**. Waiting trades a winnable fight for an unwinnable one. Let the party's own clues (Douglas's "tonight," the séance's "before the Watcher crosses") make this plain: **delay is the more dangerous path, even though night is the more dangerous hour.**

A party that has done its homework feels the squeeze exactly as intended: everything they've learned says *go into the worst possible place at the worst possible hour, right now.* That's the scenario working.

## Douglas's Room (the one sensible stop)

With Douglas fled and the house in uproar, his room is finally accessible to anyone — no Locksmith needed; the door's ajar. This is the **only** investigation that makes sense before the march, and it pays:

- If the party never tossed his room Saturday, they get it all now: the degrading journal, the **timing window and save-condition**, the church-interior **sketch with the altar marked X**.
- If they already searched it, a fresh detail: the packed suitcase is *open*, half its contents flung out — wherever he's going, he knows he isn't coming back. A pressed flower from Sarah's memorial sits on top of the case. Even now, somewhere under the Watcher's hand, Douglas is grieving.

**Keeper:** keep this tight — one scene, then push them out the door. Lingering for a second full investigation here is the night-version of the Sunday-sandbox mistake; don't allow the clock to go slack again.

## Preparation (30–60 minutes — cash in the benefits)

Before the woods, let the party assemble its loadout. This is the moment all that digging *pays*. Run down Appendix C with them; a well-prepared group should be visibly, satisfyingly armed against what's coming:

- **Salt** (for wards), **lanterns + spare oil** (the Watcher recoils from light), **rope** (to bind Douglas), **the crowbar** (to topple the altar or smash the stone), **the Luger** (five precious rounds), **flasks of lake-water** (it burns the thing), **a leather apron or heavy coats** (its grip slides off hide).
- **Ward knowledge** (Appendix A) and the **binding ritual**, if researched.
- **Allies:** **Bertrand** (guide, ward-drawer, Occult 45%) and **Ophelia** (steadies nerves, leads the binding in a third of the time). **Rupert**, if confessed and forgiven, presses his father's grimoire-notes and a crude map into their hands and begs them to end what he began.

**Keeper:** name what they *don't* have, too. A party that skipped the lake doesn't know to bring water; one that skipped the kitchen has no salt and will be drawing wards the hard way. Let the gaps bite — that's the incentive structure delivering its bill.

## The Trek to St Crispin's

The walk is part of the horror. Make the distance *felt* — this is why the church was kept far.

> Bertrand (or the map, or sheer nerve) takes you off the lawns and into the north woods. The house-lights drown behind the trees within minutes. The path is barely a path. The summer night should be alive with insects; instead the woods hold their breath. The air cools, then *chills*, until you can see it plume in your lantern-light, and a smell of stagnant water seeps up from nowhere — there is no water here. Ahead, through the trunks, a faint sick luminescence rises and falls, rises and falls, like something breathing.

**Spot Hidden** on the path: fresh prints (Douglas's), branches snapped at chest height where he tore through, and **faint chalk marks on the tree-trunks** — he has been marking this route for nights. **Navigate** keeps the pace up; failure adds dread and minutes but doesn't dead-end (Bertrand, or the luminescence itself, keeps them oriented). Drop the temperature and the silence in stages so the players *want* to be out of these woods — which means *into* the church.

## St Crispin's — The Exterior

> The church is a broken-backed thing of black stone, older than anything else on the estate, half-swallowed by the forest. A scatter of headstones leans in the long grass — and one, newer, with fresh flowers: SARAH. The door stands ajar. Light pulses through every gap in the walls, the colour of a bruise. From inside comes a sound that is almost chanting and almost an organ-note held too long, and under it, every so often, the grind of stone on stone.

**Spot Hidden (the graveyard):** the earth by **Sarah's grave** has been freshly dug and refilled. **Digging it up** turns out a cloth bundle — Douglas's spare journal and **a clean sketch of the ritual configuration inside.** **BENEFIT → Ritual layout:** the party knows exactly how the stone sits on the altar and where Douglas will stand before they cross the threshold. *(If they scouted by day, they have this already.)*

**A pre-drawn ward** on the doorframe (from a daytime scout, or drawn now before entering — Appendix A) means the Watcher cannot follow the party out this door if they retreat. Reward the foresight.

**Listen:** the chanting is in no human tongue, but a name surfaces in it again and again, mangled — *"…Sa-rah… Sa-rah…"* The Watcher is using Rupert's longing, and Sarah's memory, as the lure that holds the door open.

## St Crispin's — The Interior & the Confrontation

> Inside, the ruined nave opens around you: collapsed pews, rubble, and at the far end an intact altar ringed in guttering candles. On the altar stands a carved basalt cylinder, forearm-length, slick and dark — the **ward-stone**, lifted from the lake and set here wrong. Above it the air is *torn*: a vertical wound in reality, shimmering like heat-haze, and through it you glimpse impossible stone corridors, water falling upward, and something vast shifting in the dark beyond. And before the altar, his back to you, stands Douglas — chanting, swaying, **taller than a man should be,** his limbs gone long and his shadow longer.

SAN **1/1D6** as Douglas turns and you see what his face is becoming. Run the finale in three phases; resolutions follow.

### Phase 1 — Douglas (negotiation & leverage)

Douglas can still speak, in a voice harmonized with something deeper: *"You shouldn't have come. The bridge is almost finished. Soon I'll understand everything."*

- **Psychology:** the real Douglas is still in there, fighting, terrified of what he's becoming. He is *victim*, not villain — make sure the players feel it.
- **Appeal to his humanity (Persuade/Fast Talk, Hard).** *"You don't have to do this. Think who you were."* Success makes him **hesitate** — the chant falters, the wound above the altar flickers, an opening appears. **A party that built rapport with Douglas** (dinner, library) gets this at a **Regular** difficulty, or with a bonus die, or — at your discretion — simply *grants* the hesitation; their kindness across two days is the key that fits. *(This is the mechanical payoff for all that roleplay.)*
- **Threaten the stone (Intimidate).** Raise the crowbar over the ward-stone and Douglas/Watcher recoils in real fear: *"No! Without the stone there is neither crossing nor sealing — you would trap us all!"* **BENEFIT → Leverage:** the entity *needs* the stone intact for its own crossing, which hands the party a bargaining chip.

### Phase 2 — The Watcher manifests

As the ritual strains toward completion, the thing pushes partway through the wound. It never fully arrives — it exists *in the threshold,* half here, half elsewhere.

> It is a shape your eye refuses to keep — too many eyes, arranged wrongly, that see through every shadow at once; limbs or tentacles or something with no honest word, reaching from the tear; a cold that radiates off it in waves; and a pressure behind your own eyes as it turns its attention on you, knows you, finds you *interesting.*

SAN **1D3/1D10.** It does not speak so much as *deposit* meaning directly into the mind: *THE BRIDGE IS ALMOST WHOLE. KNOWLEDGE AWAITS THOSE WHO SERVE. SERVE, OR BE CONSUMED.*

**Its weaknesses are exactly the ones the party could have prepared for** — make the prep visibly pay:
- **Bright light** forces it to recoil; a lantern held high keeps it at bay, a sudden flare blinds it for 1D3 rounds.
- **Lake-water** burns it — a thrown flask deals real harm and makes it shriek and withdraw a limb.
- **Heavy leather** turns its grip; a character in a leather apron simply *cannot be held.*
- It **cannot cross without Douglas (the bridge) and the stone (the key).** Remove either and the crossing fails.

### Phase 3 — Resolutions

The party can win in several ways; each is gated to benefits they may have banked. Let them improvise combinations.

- **A · Reseal — return the stone to the lake (best ending).** If they learned the lake socket stands empty, they can carry the ward-stone back and sink it home. *Requires* getting the stone off the altar (Douglas/Watcher resist), then a fraught journey or a held action to submerge it — best set up by binding the entity or distracting it with light and water first. **Result:** the threshold seals for another century; the Watcher is shut out completely; **Douglas, severed while still human, collapses and survives** (recovering over days). SAN reward **+1D8.**
- **B · Collapse — destroy the stone.** Smash it (crowbar, 2 rounds), topple the altar onto it (3 people or 1 with the crowbar-lever), or hurl it into bright light while the Watcher touches it. **Result:** the portal tears shut violently, **stranding the Watcher on the far side.** Douglas survives *if pulled clear of the altar first,* otherwise he goes through with it. SAN reward **+1D6.**
- **C · Bind it (if the ritual was learned).** Three chanters, a salt circle, ~15 minutes (5 with Ophelia leading); any chanter taking damage breaks it. **Result:** the Watcher is held immobile within the circle, buying the party time to choose reseal, collapse, or simple retreat at leisure. SAN reward **+1D3,** plus whatever the final resolution grants.
- **D · Coerce it.** With real leverage — the crowbar over the stone, Douglas at gunpoint, a binding circle half-drawn — the party can bargain. It is immortal and patient; if genuinely threatened it will **release Douglas and withdraw to wait for another night** rather than risk destruction. **Result:** Douglas freed (badly traumatized), the door dormant but not sealed, a warning that *"others are watching, and I am not the only thing that waits."* SAN reward **+1D4.** *(Not a clean win — a problem deferred.)*
- **E · Fight (the costly road).** It can be done with full preparation, and is lethal without it. Douglas (Appendix B) attacks with unnatural strength; the Watcher lashes with reach and cold. The portal can't be truly closed by violence alone — but driving the manifestation back with light and lake-water long enough to **destroy the stone** (Option B) ends it. Expect blood.

### Sarah's Intervention (the earned safety net)

If the party is being overwhelmed — *and* they have grieved honestly at the memorial or the grave, or treated the family with compassion — Sarah comes.

> A young woman's shape gathers at the broken window, lit from no source, not quite here. Her voice carries into the nave, gentle and final: *"Father. Bertrand. I forgive you. Let it go. Let **me** go."*

Rupert (if present) breaks down and ceases any interference; **Bertrand** (if present) finds a moment's clarity and can take one decisive action; **Douglas** hears her and screams — *his own* scream, not the Watcher's — tearing a real opening; and the **Watcher recoils** from a soul that crossed *willingly*, a threshold it cannot force. Use this to pull a flailing party back from a TPK — but only when they've earned it. Never automatic.

# ACT FOUR — AFTERMATH

Dawn finds the woods ordinary again — birdsong, dew, the stink of stagnant water gone. Resolve the threads to fit what the party did.

**Douglas's fate.**
- *Saved* (reseal, or severed while still human): collapses unconscious, recovers over weeks, **permanently loses 1D10 SAN** and keeps fractured memories of "the other side." May return as a haunted ally — or a liability.
- *Lost* (taken through, or killed at the altar): body gone, or left as a husk. What he knew crossed with him; the Watcher learned much of our world. *A debt that may come due in a later scenario.*
- *Abandoned* (party leaves without resolving him): he completes the transformation over the following nights and becomes the Watcher's permanent servant — a recurring antagonist.

**The Merriwethers.**
- **Rupert**, shown compassion, is broken but begins to heal, and becomes a grateful patron of the SEU. Shown contempt, he does not survive the year.
- **Bertrand**, if he helped, finds his guilt finally lighter; a likely future ally and SEU contact.
- **Agnes** ensures the investigators are generously rewarded and shielded from any awkward questions about a houseguest who "left suddenly in the night."

**Rewards.**
- **Sanity** (cumulative, per Phase 3): surviving the confrontation **+1D6**; sealing or collapsing the door, saving Douglas, binding, and coercion as listed above.
- **Material:** the run of the Merriwether occult **library**; the family's financial gratitude (**$500–$1,000**, a fortune in 1924); standing within the SEU; and, if they earned it, a protective text or two to keep.
- **Knowledge:** **Cthulhu Mythos +3%** for surviving the Watcher (+2% more for those who read the ritual materials closely) — and the cold understanding that thresholds like this one exist *elsewhere.*

# APPENDIX A — Protective Symbols (Tactical Reference)

Learned from ***Geometries of Protection*** (Library). **Quick study (30 min, Occult):** one application on a success, two on a Hard, all three on an Extreme; a *failed* roll produces flawed symbols that look right and don't work (don't tell the player). **Thorough study (2 hrs, Occult with bonus die):** all of the first application even on a failure, more on better successes, **plus the Principle of Opposition** (below). Reading the book costs **1 SAN.** **Salt as the medium makes every drawing roll one level easier** — the reason the cook's salt matters.

**1 · The Elder Sign (stationary ward).** 5 min; any marking medium; Occult to draw. Creates a point no entity will willingly cross — on a doorway (it can't pass), a floor-spot (it can't step there), or a person (it can't touch them directly; fades from skin after ~1 hour). Protects a ~5-ft radius. The entity can ruin it indirectly (knock the object over, smear the chalk, send Douglas to scuff it). *Does not stop Douglas* (he reads as human) unless the Principle of Opposition applies.

**2 · The Containment Circle (binding perimeter).** 15 min (rushed to 5 = one level harder); a *continuous* line, salt ideal (chalk Hard; blood works at 1 HP per 10 ft); 6–30 ft across; Occult to close. Nothing supernatural crosses it **either way** while unbroken — trap the entity inside, or shelter the party within (but they can't leave either; ranged attacks and thrown lake-water still cross). **Any** break in the line ends it instantly. Douglas, human enough to cross, can scuff it from inside.

**3 · The Banishment Glyph (aggressive ward).** 10 min; salt **plus** a second medium; ~2 ft across; **Hard** Occult (Regular if the drawer did the 2-hour thorough study). An entity within 10 ft must make a POW roll or be driven back 20 ft; even on a success it suffers and attacks at one level harder near the glyph. Forces retreat; doesn't contain or damage.

**Principle of Opposition (thorough study only).** *"That which repels the outsider equally repels what the outsider has touched."* Elder Signs and circles **also affect transformed Douglas** — most strongly after midnight, when he's furthest from human. **The non-lethal Douglas solution:** contain him in a circle and hold him to dawn, when the Watcher's grip slackens and he becomes reachable again.

**Failure modes.** *Failure:* symbol looks correct, works for one round, then fails at the worst moment. *Fumble:* the flawed symbol *attracts* the entity's attention instead. **Keeper:** never reveal a failed drawing roll until the symbol is tested — the doubt ("did I get it right?") is the tension.

**Quick table for the church:**

| To… | Use | Roll | Time |
|---|---|---|---|
| Stop the Watcher following you out a door | Elder Sign on the frame | Occult | 5 min |
| Shield one person from its touch | Elder Sign on chest/brow | Occult | 5 min |
| Make a safe redoubt to plan/wait | Containment Circle around the party | Occult (Hard w/o salt) | 15 min |
| Deny it the altar/stone | Containment Circle around the altar | Occult (Hard w/o salt) | 15 min |
| Trap it | Containment Circle around the entity | Occult (Hard w/o salt) | 15 min |
| Drive it off an area | Banishment Glyph | Hard Occult | 10 min |
| Subdue Douglas non-lethally | Containment Circle (after midnight) | Occult | 15 min |

# APPENDIX B — Antagonists

**Douglas Kimball — the Bridge** *(partially transformed)*
STR 16 · DEX 14 · HP 14. **Claw** 1D6+1D4, or **grapple.** Unnatural strength and speed.
- **Weakness — his name.** Addressed as himself with a Psychology success, he hesitates: attacks against him gain a bonus die for one round.
- **Reduced to 0 HP:** he collapses, the Watcher's connection severs, the portal destabilizes. (Whether this *saves* or *kills* him depends on timing and whether he's pulled clear — see Act Four.)
- Before midnight he is more man than monster; after 2 a.m. he is more the Watcher's than his own.

**The Watcher** *(partial manifestation only)*
Cannot be truly killed while the portal exists; **destroy the portal (the stone) and it is banished.**
- **Tentacle** 1D8, reach. **Cold aura:** anyone within 10 ft takes 1 HP/round.
- **Light:** a held lantern halts its advance; a flare blinds it 1D3 rounds.
- **Lake-water:** 1D6 per flask-hit; it recoils.
- **Leather:** cannot maintain a grapple on a character protected by heavy hide.
- **Bridge & key:** without Douglas it cannot cross; without the stone the portal collapses.

**Allies (if recruited):** **Bertrand** — Occult 45%, can draw one ward correctly, knows the woods. **Ophelia** — no combat use, but her presence steadies the party (Keeper's discretion: a bonus die to one PC's first Sanity-pressed roll per scene) and lets her **lead the binding ritual in 5 minutes instead of 15.**
